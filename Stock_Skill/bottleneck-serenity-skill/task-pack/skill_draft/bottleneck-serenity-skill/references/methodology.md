# bottleneck-serenity-skill methodology

## 1. The unit of analysis

The unit of analysis is not an industry and not initially a company. It is an **economically necessary function inside a changing system**.

A trend becomes investable only through this chain:

```text
funded demand
→ system architecture
→ mandatory functions
→ scarce qualified capacity
→ bargaining power
→ company-level economic exposure
→ per-share cash-flow capture
→ market expectation gap
→ catalyst and holding period
```

The framework therefore distinguishes four questions that are often collapsed:

1. Is the system changing?
2. Where is the limiting constraint?
3. Who captures the rent generated by that constraint?
4. Is the rent underpriced in a tradable security?

A correct answer to question 2 does not imply a correct answer to question 4.

## 2. Four-gate architecture

### Gate A — Constraint reality

A node is a verified constraint when most of the following are true:

- downstream demand is funded or mandated;
- the function is architecturally necessary;
- qualified demand approaches or exceeds capacity;
- suppliers are concentrated or capacity is geographically constrained;
- qualification, permitting, yield learning, or switching is slow;
- substitution requires meaningful redesign, performance sacrifice, or regulatory approval;
- customers cannot cheaply defer the entire downstream system.

Useful evidence includes allocation, lead times, utilization, backlog, price changes, qualification disclosures, permit queues, import/export concentration, customer redesign commentary, and equipment order books.

### Gate B — Scarcity duration

Scarcity is temporary by definition. Estimate how it ends:

- new capacity;
- yield improvement;
- substitution or thrift;
- vertical integration;
- demand destruction;
- policy intervention;
- inventory liquidation;
- architecture transition.

The key variable is not “is it scarce?” but:

```text
monetizable_runway = scarcity_duration - company_monetization_lag
```

A six-year mine-development bottleneck can be valuable; a six-month component shortage with an 18-month qualification and ramp path may not be.

### Gate C — Shareholder rent capture

Scarcity creates system-level value, but several actors may capture it:

- the bottleneck owner;
- a buyer with fixed-price contracts;
- an equipment supplier expanding capacity;
- a substitute;
- a distributor controlling allocation;
- labour, regulators, landlords, creditors, or governments;
- no one, because capex and dilution consume the profit.

The public security captures rent only when the relevant asset is inside the listed entity, exposure is material, pricing or volume reaches reported economics, financing is survivable, and per-share free cash flow increases.

### Gate D — Expectations and price

A bottleneck can be fully known and overvalued. Compare current price with the evidence-weighted distribution of future outcomes. The edge exists when:

- the dependency is poorly mapped or sits across analyst silos;
- reported data lag qualification or contracts;
- consensus models use obsolete architecture or capacity assumptions;
- the company is too small, foreign, complex, or segment-obscured for broad coverage;
- a concrete catalyst can move estimates;
- valuation does not require the optimistic case.

## 3. Three clocks

Always build three separate clocks.

### Physical scarcity clock

How long until supply, substitution, policy, or demand response resolves the constraint?

Inputs:

- construction and equipment lead time;
- qualification duration;
- yield ramp;
- permit and interconnection time;
- labour and input constraints;
- announced versus funded capacity;
- probability-weighted cancellations.

### Company monetization clock

How long until the candidate converts the constraint into per-share economics?

Inputs:

- design win to production;
- contract start and volume ramp;
- available capacity and yield;
- price reset timing;
- receivables, inventory, and capex;
- financing and dilution.

### Market discovery clock

How long until the market notices and prices the evidence?

Inputs:

- reporting cadence;
- analyst coverage;
- customer disclosures;
- estimate revisions;
- index inclusion or liquidity events;
- conference and qualification milestones;
- current narrative and ownership.

The highest-quality setup often has a long physical scarcity clock, a shorter monetization clock, and a still-open discovery window.

## 4. Constraint archetypes

### 4.1 Resource or feedstock owner

Examples: deposits, refined materials, specialty gases, substrates.

Check:

- reserve/grade and extraction rights;
- conversion capacity, not merely geological abundance;
- price index and contract resets;
- sovereign and environmental constraints;
- customer qualification;
- capex and dilution.

### 4.2 Qualified component supplier

Examples: high-performance components with long design cycles.

Check:

- sole-source versus second-source status;
- content per system;
- yields and available capacity;
- customer concentration and bargaining;
- redesign path;
- warranty and reliability exposure.

### 4.3 Capacity unlocker

Examples: equipment, test, metrology, automation, engineering services.

This may be superior to the bottleneck owner when the industry spends heavily to relieve scarcity. Check whether expansion orders are multi-cycle, whether the equipment itself has substitutes, and whether service revenue compounds.

### 4.4 Standard, software, or workflow tollbooth

Examples: mandatory design software, certification, identity, data, exchanges, or transaction rails.

Check switching costs, interoperability, regulatory status, pricing model, and whether open-source or customer-built alternatives can emerge.

### 4.5 Permit, grid, location, or logistics right

Examples: interconnection rights, power capacity, ports, pipeline access, spectrum, zoning.

The economic asset may be a right or queue position rather than physical land. Verify transferability, duration, regulatory return caps, and who legally owns it.

### 4.6 Substitute winner

The best equity may benefit from eliminating the bottleneck rather than owning it. Test performance parity, qualification timing, cost curve, customer incentives, and whether substitution creates a new bottleneck elsewhere.

### 4.7 Demand absorber

A supplier with qualified spare capacity can capture displaced demand during a shortage. Confirm that capacity is real, economical, and compatible—not nominal nameplate capacity.

### 4.8 Public proxy

When the true constraint is private, state-owned, or uninvestable, identify listed beneficiaries and estimate exposure purity. Do not pretend the proxy owns the chokepoint.

## 5. Rent-capture mechanics

### 5.1 Small BOM share, large system consequence

A low-cost component that can halt a high-value system may support pricing power because downstream customers prefer paying more to delaying the entire project. This only works if:

- contracts permit repricing;
- the supplier can ship incremental units;
- the customer cannot qualify a substitute quickly;
- the supplier is not regulated or captive;
- the price increase is not absorbed by distributors or fixed-price obligations.

### 5.2 Capacity is as important as price

A company with no spare capacity may enjoy theoretical scarcity while producing no additional volume. Expansion may require years of capex and equity issuance. Model:

```text
incremental_equity_value
= incremental_volume × realized_price × incremental_margin
- expansion_capex
- working_capital
- financing_cost
- dilution_cost
```

### 5.3 Appropriability and complementary assets

Innovation creators do not always capture innovation value. Manufacturing, distribution, standards, customer access, service networks, and regulatory approvals can be more valuable than the invention. Map who owns these complementary assets.

### 5.4 Customer power

A concentrated, creditworthy customer improves demand certainty but can reduce supplier economics through:

- dual sourcing;
- cost-down clauses;
- prepayments tied to low prices;
- vertical integration;
- ownership of tooling or IP;
- cancellation and volume-flex terms.

Treat “major customer” as both evidence and risk.

## 6. Capital-cycle discipline

Every profitable constraint invites response. Monitor:

- announced and funded capacity;
- equipment orders;
- new entrants;
- customer prepayments;
- government subsidies;
- inventory growth;
- falling lead times;
- declining spot premiums;
- rising asset growth and financing needs;
- technology substitution.

The exit may occur before reported revenue peaks because markets discount the resolution of scarcity. Define a **constraint half-life** and update it quarterly.

## 7. Information edge

Supply-chain alpha can arise because analysts and investors cover companies, sectors, and countries in silos. News at a large customer may diffuse slowly to a smaller supplier. However:

- published anomalies often weaken after discovery;
- social-media dissemination can close the window rapidly;
- small-cap price impact can be mistaken for information;
- the same evidence may already be embedded in options, ownership, or valuation.

The framework requires an explicit statement of why the market may be wrong and how that claim could be tested.

## 8. Lifecycle map

Classify every candidate:

1. `DISCOVERY` — plausible constraint, weak evidence.
2. `QUALIFICATION` — design wins or approvals, limited revenue.
3. `CONTRACTED_RAMP` — funded demand and visible ramp.
4. `MARGIN_REALIZATION` — price/volume reaches financials.
5. `CONSENSUS_ADOPTION` — coverage and estimates catch up.
6. `CAPACITY_RESPONSE` — new supply and substitutes accelerate.
7. `NORMALIZATION` — scarcity rent decays.

Position logic must match stage. A small research position may be rational in qualification; a larger core position requires evidence and funding; consensus adoption may require harvesting rather than adding.

## 9. Bayesian hypothesis register

For each thesis maintain:

- prior probability based on historical analogues;
- evidence item and likelihood direction;
- posterior confidence range;
- alternative hypotheses;
- disconfirming evidence;
- next observable event.

Do not convert subjective scores into fake statistical probabilities. Use broad bands:

- low confidence: 20–40%;
- medium: 40–65%;
- high: 65–80%;
- exceptional: above 80% only with contracted, independently verified, financeable economics.

## 10. Core equations

### Monetizable runway

```text
MR = scarcity_P50_months - monetization_lag_months
```

### Revenue exposure

```text
addressable_revenue
= system_units × content_per_unit × attainable_share × realized_price
```

### Per-share capture

```text
per_share_FCF_delta
= (addressable_revenue × incremental_FCF_margin - funding_cost)
  / fully_diluted_shares
```

### Opportunity quality

```text
core_quality
= geometric_mean(
    structural_constraint,
    rent_capture,
    mispricing_timing,
    evidence_quality,
    investability
  )
```

Geometric aggregation encodes the principle that a near-zero dimension should collapse the overall result.

### Scenario asymmetry

```text
expected_return = Σ(probability_i × return_i)
downside_reference = abs(bear_return)
asymmetry = expected_return / max(downside_reference, floor)
```

Use this only after validating assumptions and probabilities.

## 11. The key distinction

The framework's central sentence is:

> A bottleneck is a system fact. An investment is a security claim on the cash flows generated by that fact at a particular price and time.
